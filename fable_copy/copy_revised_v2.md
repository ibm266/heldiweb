# Heldi homepage — revised copy v2

Section-by-section revision of `current_copy_v1.md`, audited against four frameworks:

- **SHARP** — mental availability, category entry points, distinctive assets
- **KELLER** — salience → meaning → credibility/feeling → resonance
- **MORGAN** — lighthouse identity, sacrifice, overcommitment
- **DTC** — one job per page, benefit-first, message priority matches scroll order

Rules applied throughout: minimal text, every line earns its place, no em dashes, UK compliance (composition facts + "protein contributes to the maintenance of muscle mass" only).

Legend: ✅ keep · ✏️ revise · ➕ add · ❌ cut

## 15. "The truth about protein" page ➕ (educational anchor, linked from pouch section)

**Audit:** The Pillar 1 bridge. The homepage states the gap in one line; this page proves it. Honest-expert voice throughout. Copy blocks are short because visuals carry the argument; every H2 leads with its answer for AI extraction. Target query: "how much protein in dal" with "how to add protein to Indian food" as secondary.

### Page title
> # The truth about protein in Indian food

### Opening block (the extractable answer)
> A standard bowl of dal has 5 to 7g of protein, not the 18 some blogs claim. A full day of home-cooked vegetarian Indian food typically delivers 35 to 45g, while an active adult needs 1.2 to 1.6g per kilo of body weight, which for most people is 75g or more. That gap, eaten daily for decades, is the problem nobody talks about.

### Section: Your day on a plate
**Copy:**
> Here is a normal day, weighed honestly.
*(That's the whole copy block. The visual does the talking.)*

**Visual (interactive, the centrepiece):** a horizontal day timeline in the site's sticker-card style. Chai + toast, then dal chawal, then sabzi roti with dahi. Tap each meal and its true grams pop onto a running counter (same pop animation as the stir gallery). The counter fills a vertical "protein meter" with a marked target line at 75g. The day ends at ~40g with the meter visibly half empty. Final frame: "The gap: 35g. Every single day."

### Section: How much protein is in dal, really
**Copy:**
> An 80g cooked serving of dal gives you 5 to 7g of protein (McCance and Widdowson). The 18 to 22g figures online come from measuring dry lentils, then quietly forgetting that nobody eats 100g of dry dal in one sitting.

**Visual:** two bowls side by side, sticker-card style. "Internet dal: 18g" with a gold pill, crossed out with the terracotta marker stroke. "Your dal: 6g" beside it. Underneath, small: "The internet weighed it dry. You eat it cooked."

### Section: Why dal alone can't do the job
**Copy:**
> Protein is a team of nine essential amino acids, and your body needs all nine at once. Legumes run low on one player (methionine), grains run low on another (lysine). Dal chawal pairs them, which is genuinely clever, but pairing fixes the quality problem, not the quantity problem. You are still eating 6g servings toward a 75g day. Whey brings all nine players, which is why it is measured as one of the most complete proteins there is.

**Visual (fun, ownable):** a cricket team lineup of nine players in the ink illustration style. Dal's team walks out with two players asleep on the bench (labelled methionine and lysine gaps for dal and rice respectively). Then the dal chawal combined XI: all nine standing, but tiny. Then whey's team: all nine, full size. Caption: "Dal and rice complete the team. They just turn up small."

**Interactive option:** tap dal / rice / dal chawal / paneer / whey chips and the team lineup rearranges live. Cheap to build, very shareable.

### Section: What the gap does over decades
**Copy:**
> Adults lose muscle gradually from their 30s onward, and research shows South Asians carry less muscle mass to begin with. Low daily protein accelerates the slide; strength work and adequate protein slow it. This is why protein contributes to the maintenance of muscle mass is the claim that matters most after 50. The gap is invisible at 30. It is the difference between carrying your own shopping at 75, or not.

**Visual:** a single declining line chart in brand style: muscle mass vs age. Two lines from age 35: "low protein, low activity" falling steeply, "enough protein + staying active" falling gently. No fear-mongering styling, just two honest slopes. Terracotta and ink.

*(Compliance: the steep line is labelled with the behaviour, never with a disease outcome. No "prevents sarcopenia". Cite the South Asian muscle-mass research and NHS/BNF figures at the bottom.)*

### Section: How to actually close the gap
**Copy:**
> The honest answers, in order: eat more dahi and paneer, make the dal thicker than the recipe says, lift something heavy twice a week. And if you want the simplest route, stir Heldi into what you already cook: 10g per spoonful, no recipe changes, as part of a varied and balanced diet and a healthy lifestyle.

*(One Heldi mention on the whole page. That restraint is what makes the page citable.)*

**Visual:** the protein meter from the top of the page returns, and each honest fix nudges it up: +dahi, +thicker dal, +2 tbsp Heldi. The meter finally crosses the 75g line on the last nudge. Ends on the waitlist CTA.

### FAQ block (for AI extraction)
Four questions, answers 40 to 80 words each, reusing homepage FAQ copy where it overlaps:
1. How much protein is in a bowl of dal?
2. Is dal a complete protein?
3. How much protein do I need a day?
4. How do I add protein to Indian food without shakes?

### Footer of page
