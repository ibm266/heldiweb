## FAQ
**Headline:** The questions we hear most.

**Is whey protein vegetarian?**
> Yes, and it is made without animal rennet. Whey is the pale liquid left when milk curdles, the same one you see when paneer is made at home. We simply filter it to concentrate the protein and gently dry it into a fine powder. Nothing added, just the part of milk that has always been there.

**Why not just drink a protein shake?**
> You can, if you like them. Most of our parents don't. Heldi hits the same protein number through the meals you were going to eat anyway. Same dal, same raita, ten more grams per bowl. Nothing new to swallow, nothing to give up.

**Will my food taste different?**
> No. The spices are designed to disappear into the dish, not sit on top of the flavour. Heldi blends clean into what you already cook. No chalky film, no protein-shake aftertaste.

**How do I use it?**
> Once the pot is done cooking and has cooled a little, stir the powder straight into the full dal, curry or raita and mix it through. Or leave the jar on the table and let each person add as much as they like to their own bowl. One to two spoonfuls per dish is the sweet spot.

**Can I use it in dishes that are not on the pouch?**
> Yes. Anything with a gravy, a dal or a yoghurt base works: sambar, kadhi, korma, even a chaat with dahi on top. If a spoon can stir it, Heldi can disappear into it.

**Is it safe for kids?**
> Yes. Whey is a part of milk, the same protein kids already get from dahi and paneer, and protein is part of every balanced diet. Nothing artificial, no sweeteners, no caffeine. If the family pot gets a spoonful, everyone eats from it. Growing kids get what they need from normal meals, so there is no need to add extra to their bowls.

**Is it safe for parents and grandparents?**
> Heldi is designed for the whole table. Protein contributes to the maintenance of muscle mass, and the isolate is 99% lactose-free, 100% vegetarian and free from added sugar, preservatives and gluten.

# Our story

**Heldi began with a word my nani made up.**

Ask her if the food was healthy and she would smile and say it was heldi. Not healthy in the clinical sense. Heldi the way home-cooked food is: warm, familiar, made with care, made for you.

That word stayed with me.

I'm Mihir. I grew up in a vegetarian house on dal, sabzi, raita and chai, and I loved all of it. Then training became a big part of my life, and the food of my childhood started slipping off my plate. Not because I stopped loving it. Because the numbers didn't add up. The meals I grew up on are rich in almost everything except protein.

I didn't want bland fitness food, and I didn't want another shake. I wanted the food I loved to do one more thing for me. So I made Heldi: protein that disappears into the dishes we already cook.

Then I realised who really needed it. My parents. They will never drink a protein shake, and they will never stop eating dal. As we get older, protein matters more, not less. Protein contributes to the maintenance of muscle mass, and I want as many long walks with my mum and dad as I can get. Their plate is part of that plan.

In a lot of desi homes, supplements carry a stigma. Powders are for gym boys, not for the family table. Heldi is built to be the opposite. Food first. Family friendly. Whey, the part of milk that has always been there, stirred into recipes that never change.

Heldi is not here to replace tradition. It is here to back it up. The food we grew up with is already beautiful. Heldi simply helps it carry more of what we need.

# The truth about protein in Indian food

**Opening (extractable answer):**
> A standard bowl of dal has 5 to 7g of protein, not the 18 some blogs claim. A full day of home-cooked vegetarian Indian food typically delivers 35 to 45g, while an active adult needs 75g or more. That gap, eaten daily for decades, is the problem nobody talks about.

## How much protein is in dal, really
> An 80g cooked serving of dal gives you 5 to 7g of protein. The 18 to 22g figures online come from weighing dry lentils, then quietly forgetting that nobody eats 100g of dry dal in one sitting.

*Visual: two bowls side by side. "Internet dal: 18g" crossed out in terracotta. "Your dal: 6g." Small caption: "The internet weighed it dry. You eat it cooked."*

## Why dal alone can't do the job
> Protein is a team of nine essential amino acids, and your body needs all nine at once. Legumes run low on one player, grains run low on another. Dal chawal pairs them, which is genuinely clever, but pairing fixes quality, not quantity. You are still eating 6g servings toward a 75g day. Whey brings all nine, full size.

*Visual: cricket team lineup, ink illustration style. Dal's XI with methionine asleep on the bench; rice missing lysine; the dal chawal combined XI all present but tiny; whey's XI all nine, full size. Caption: "Dal and rice complete the team. They just turn up small." Interactive option: tap food chips (dal / rice / dal chawal / paneer / whey), lineup rearranges live.*

## What the gap does over decades
> Adults lose muscle gradually from their 30s onward, and research shows South Asians carry less muscle to begin with. Enough protein and staying active slow the slide. This is why "protein contributes to the maintenance of muscle mass" is the claim that matters most after 50. Invisible at 30. At 75, it is the difference between carrying your own shopping, or not.

*Visual: one line chart, two slopes from age 35: "low protein, low activity" falls steeply; "enough protein + staying active" falls gently. Terracotta and ink, no fear styling. Lines labelled with behaviours, never diseases.*

## How to close the gap
> The honest answers, in order: eat more dahi and paneer, make the dal thicker than the recipe says, lift something heavy twice a week. And if you want the simplest route, stir Heldi into what you already cook: 10g per spoonful, no recipe changes, as part of a varied and balanced diet and a healthy lifestyle.

## FAQ
**How much protein is in a bowl of dal?**
> About 5 to 7g for a standard 80g cooked serving. Bigger bowls reach 9 or 10g. The 18g+ figures online describe dry weight, which nobody eats in one sitting.

**Is dal a complete protein?**
> Not on its own. Dal is low in methionine, one of the nine essential amino acids. Pairing it with rice or roti covers the full set, but the combined meal is still modest in total protein.

**How much protein do I need a day?**
> Active adults and adults over 50 benefit from roughly 1.2 to 1.6g per kilo of body weight per day. For a 65kg person, that is 78 to 104g. Most home-cooked vegetarian days land well under half of that.

**How do I add protein to Indian food without shakes?**
> Add dahi or paneer to more meals, thicken your dal, and stir a clean whey isolate like Heldi straight into gravies, dals and yoghurt dishes. One spoonful adds 10g without changing the recipe.
