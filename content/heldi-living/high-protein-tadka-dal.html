Dal is the daily soap of a desi kitchen. Same time, same pot, and nobody actually wants the story to change. This is the masoor dal my mother has made for as long as I can remember, with one quiet edit at the end that takes each bowl from about 12g of protein to about 22g. The dal does not change. The tadka does not change. Papa, the house's chief dal loyalist and its hardest judge, has signed off. That is the whole pitch.

At a glance

  • Serves: 4 proper main-course bowls
  • Prep: 10 minutes
  • Cook: 35 minutes
  • Protein per bowl: about 12g from the dal, about 22g with one heaped tablespoon of Heldi Khana stirred in

An honest note before anyone checks our maths, because on this site someone always should. A standard 80g side serving of dal carries 5 to 7g of protein. These are generous main-course bowls, 50g of dry masoor each, so you start from around 12g. Heldi takes each bowl past 20.

Ingredients

The dal

  • 200g red lentils (masoor dal), rinsed until the water runs clear
  • 900ml water
  • 1/2 tsp haldi
  • 1 tsp fine salt, or to taste

The tadka

  • 2 tbsp ghee
  • 1 tsp jeera
  • 3 cloves garlic, sliced
  • 1 small onion, finely chopped
  • 1 tomato, chopped
  • 1/2 tsp Kashmiri chilli powder, or to taste

At the table

  • 4 heaped tbsp Heldi Khana, one per bowl
  • Fresh coriander, chopped

Method

  1. Put the rinsed masoor in a pot with the water, haldi and salt. Bring to a boil, then simmer uncovered for 20 to 25 minutes until completely soft, skimming any foam early on.
  2. Whisk the dal briefly to your preferred texture. Loose and rustic or smooth and creamy, your house rules apply.
  3. Heat the ghee in a small pan. Add the jeera and let it crackle.
  4. Add the garlic and cook until golden, then the onion until soft, then the tomato and chilli powder. Cook until the tomato collapses and the ghee separates at the edges.
  5. Pour the tadka over the dal and stir it through. Taste for salt.
  6. Take the pot off the heat and let it settle for a minute or two, until it has stopped bubbling.
  7. Ladle into bowls and stir one heaped tablespoon of Heldi Khana into each until it disappears. Top with coriander.

Notes

Toor dal works the same way, it just needs longer: 35 to 40 minutes on the hob or 3 whistles in a pressure cooker. The dal keeps in the fridge for three days and freezes well; add the Heldi to the bowl after reheating, not before freezing. For kids, the pot itself is family food, so a shared spoonful is fine, but growing kids get what they need from normal meals and do not need extra added to their bowls. For heat, everything is in the chilli powder, so adjust there.

Where Heldi fits

One heaped tablespoon of Heldi Khana is a 12g serving that adds 10.4g of complete protein to a bowl, taking this dal from about 12g to about 22g. Stir it in once the bowl has stopped bubbling, or leave the jar on the table and let everyone add their own. Frequently asked questions

When do I add the Heldi?

After cooking, once the dal has stopped bubbling. A minute or two off the heat is enough. Very high heat straight off the boil can make any milk protein clump, so let the pot settle first, then stir the spoonful in until it disappears. Stirring into individual bowls at the table works just as well.

Can I stir it into the whole pot instead of each bowl?

Yes. Once the pot is off the heat and has settled, stir in one heaped tablespoon per person and mix it through. Per-bowl dosing just means everyone controls their own amount, which in most desi households prevents at least one argument.

Does this work with toor or moong dal?

Yes. The Heldi step is identical for any dal: cook as normal, let it settle off the heat, stir in a heaped tablespoon per bowl. Only the cooking time of the dal itself changes. Toor needs 35 to 40 minutes or a pressure cooker; split moong is quicker than masoor.

Will it change the taste?

No. The blend is built to disappear into exactly this kind of dish, and the cumin in it is already in your tadka. No chalky film, no protein-shake aftertaste. Our internal standard is simple: if the cook can taste the difference, it has failed.
